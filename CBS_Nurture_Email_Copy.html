A2

Point of view (Day 3)

Goal: earn attention with one specific, useful insight. Still no hard ask.
Subject options
  1. The 15 feet nobody owns
  2. Your bottleneck probably is not a machine
  3. Where the week actually goes
Preview options
  1. One thing we see in almost every plant.
  2. It is rarely where you would look first.
Body

Walk almost any plant and the real delay is not inside a single step. It is in the handoff between two of them. The 15 feet and 15 minutes where a job sits, nobody owns it, and it never shows up on a report.

We see it constantly. A team is sure the constraint is a machine or a headcount problem, so they go buy capacity they already have. The actual fix is deciding who owns the gap between two steps, and measuring the wait there like it matters.

Put a name on that handoff. Make the wait visible. That is usually worth more than the next capital request, and it costs nothing.

Reply and tell us where your work piles up. We read every one.

[Read: where operations quietly lose time]
A3

Proof (Day 7)

Goal: make it real with a specific, anonymized result. First soft ask.
Subject options
  1. Same people, a week of capacity back
  2. We did not add anyone
  3. The fix was free
Preview options
  1. What it looked like on the floor.
  2. No new hires, no new machines.
Body

Here is what that looks like in practice.

A manufacturer came to us with on-time delivery under 80 percent and a team already on overtime. The obvious move was to add people. We did not.

A week on the floor showed most of the delay was sitting between two handoffs, not inside any step. We changed who owned that handoff and made the wait visible to everyone on it. On-time delivery was back above 95 percent within a quarter, same headcount.

The point is not the number. It is that the problem was in a place nobody was looking, because nobody owned it.

If that sounds like your operation, it is worth a conversation.

B2

Go deeper (Day 2)

Goal: deepen the problem the checklist surfaced.
Subject options
  1. The gaps that pass internal review and fail the audit
  2. It is rarely the paperwork
  3. Where AS9100 actually breaks
Preview options
  1. Three gaps auditors find fast.
  2. Look at the boxes you skipped.
Body

Now that you have the checklist, here is what trips teams up most. It is rarely the documentation. It is whether the documented process is the one actually running on the floor.

Three gaps show up again and again:

Process control that lives on paper but not in practice.
Corrective actions that close without fixing the root cause.
A supply chain held to a looser standard than your own floor.

Auditors find these fast, because the evidence does not match the day to day. Run back through your checklist with that lens. The items you hesitated on are probably one of these three.

B3

What good looks like (Day 5)

Goal: show the path from gap to ready, with brief proof.
Subject options
  1. Nothing to hide on audit week
  2. From scramble to ready
  3. The teams that pass without panic
Preview options
  1. Without stopping production to do it.
  2. Readiness that holds when you are busy.
Body

Knowing the gaps is one thing. Closing them while still shipping product is another. Here is what good looks like.

Strong teams do not run compliance as a side project. They build the standard into how the work already runs, so the process you follow every day is the one an auditor would see. No special behavior for audit week, because there is nothing to hide.

We get teams there by working on the floor with your people, not by handing over a binder. The result holds up when you are busy, which is exactly when readiness usually slips.

Want to see where you stand? That is a short conversation.
