Point of view (Day 3)

Goal: earn attention with one specific, useful insight. Still no hard ask.
Subject options
  1. More dashboards, same problems
  2. One number beats ten dashboards
  3. You have more data than ever. Is anything better?
Preview options
  1. Operational excellence is not a data problem.
  2. The fix is fewer, sharper metrics.
Body

Most plants are not short on data. There are dashboards, reports, and KPIs everywhere, the number of things being tracked keeps climbing, and performance does not move with it. More data has not made anyone faster.

Operational excellence does not come from more data. It comes from fewer, sharper metrics a team actually acts on: the one or two numbers that tell you today whether the operation is winning, and trigger a decision the moment they move.

A good metric passes four tests: simple enough to explain without a cheat sheet, visible every day where the work happens, tied to a behavior your team can actually influence, and clear about what to do the moment it moves outside normal. Anything that fails one of those is reporting, not managing. A number nobody looks at until month-end is not a metric. It is a receipt.

The article below walks through how to design metrics that pass those tests, plus the fastest comprehension check we know: ask your team what they would do if the chart went red.

Reply with the one number your team checks every day. We will tell you whether it is the one that matters.

Proof (Day 7)

Goal: make it real with a specific, anonymized result. First soft ask.
Subject options
  1. 110 units late. Then zero.
  2. They were working hard and going nowhere

Preview options

  2. Same plant, a system to see the work.
Body

Here is what that looks like in practice.

An industrial manufacturer came to us under real cost and delivery pressure. Customer volumes were climbing, they could not keep up, and the team was working hard with nothing to tell them whether they were winning or losing that day. No scoreboard, just past-due complaints.

We put in a visual management system: one KPI board on the floor, a daily stand-up to review yesterday's numbers, and a root-cause fix the moment a target was missed. Instead of waiting for month-end, they closed the books every day.

One product line went from 110 units past due to zero in under five months. Another nearly doubled weekly output, from 10 units to 21, with throughput time cut 30 percent. Plant-wide work in process dropped by half, all from seeing the work clearly and acting on it the same day.

If your team is working hard without a clear picture of the day, that is a conversation worth having.

Body

Now that you have the checklist, here is what trips teams up most. It is rarely the documentation. It is whether the documented process is the one actually running on the floor.

Three gaps show up again and again:

Process control that lives on paper but not in practice.
Corrective actions that close without fixing the root cause.
A supply chain held to a looser standard than your own floor.

Auditors find these fast, because the evidence does not match the day to day. Run back through your checklist with that lens. The items you hesitated on are probably one of these three.

B3

What good looks like (Day 5)

Goal: show the path from gap to ready, with brief proof.
Subject options
  1. AS9100 in months, not years
  2. A quality system that is not a binder
  3. Right-sized, not reams of paperwork
Preview options
  1. How a stretched team got there fast.
  2. The QMS woven in, not bolted on.
Body

Knowing your gaps is one thing. Building a system that closes them, without drowning in paperwork, is what good looks like.

A growing space-hardware company came to us needing AS9100 certification on a tight timeline, with a small team, no dedicated quality function, and everyone already wearing three hats. The usual playbook, reams of procedures nobody follows, was a non-starter.

We used lean thinking to right-size it: keep what adds value, cut the rest, and weave the quality system into how the business already runs. A simple dashboard kept leadership accountable week to week, so even small pockets of time moved the work forward.

After a concentrated four-month surge, their initial certification audit came back with a recommendation for certification, on a system that was integrated, not bolted on. In their COO's words, the results speak for themselves.

If certification is on your timeline and the team is already stretched, the right-sized path is worth a short conversation.
